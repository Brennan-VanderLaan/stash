Where your $4 goes

Every Pro subscription pays for a stack of vendor bills, payment fees, taxes, and human time. This page is the honest version — not "we save you $1,000 a month" marketing copy, an actual cost ledger. We'd rather be transparent than hand-wavy.

All figures below are illustrative typical-month estimates, not contractual. AWS line items are total fixed costs (compute + storage are shared across every active user), so per-tenant cost depends on how many of you there are. AI + B2 lines vary by how much each tenant actually uses the AI surfaces and how much they store. Each row carries today's small-scale floor and a generous upper bound for the next growth tier — "what we'd be paying once we need to scale out before revenue catches up." See "What changes as we grow" below for the full scale-up arc.

How the free tier is funded

The free tier is not magic. Every free user occupies disk + serves bandwidth + costs the same B2 backup pennies as a Pro user. That cost has to come from somewhere — and the only "somewhere" is active subscribers.

The math:

  • A free user can hold 100 MB of storage total (flat, not per-month). At EBS gp3's $0.08/GB-month plus that user's B2 backup slice, that's roughly $0.01–$0.05 per free user per month. Cheap individually; adds up linearly.
  • The fixed AWS hosting overhead today is about $30–$50 / month total (prod t3.small + staging t3.micro + EBS + bandwidth). Pro subscriptions have to clear this collectively before anything else funds. At the next scale tier (RDS, ALB, bigger instances) it jumps to $80–$300+ / month — see "What changes as we grow" below for the full arc.
  • Each Pro subscription leaves a remainder of about $0.50–$1.00 after vendor + AI + tax + labor. After the fixed hosting overhead is amortised across the paying base, that remainder is what funds free-tier storage + reinvestment buffer.
  • So at low subscriber counts the free tier is genuinely tight: 5–10 Pro subs cover today's fixed hosting + maybe 20–50 free users. When we cross the scale-out threshold and need RDS + a bigger instance, that ratio resets — we'll need more Pros covering a bigger fixed cost before free-tier capacity expands again. Per-user marginal cost stays low; the steps happen at the fixed-overhead line.
  • As paid membership grows past those step boundaries, the per-Pro fixed-cost slice drops sharply and free-tier capacity grows with it. As paid membership shrinks, the free tier has to shrink too. We will not silently absorb the cost — that's how products end up shutting down their free tier with two weeks' notice (Encircle, December 2025). Every change to the free-tier caps gets posted here first with the math.

If we ever need to change the free-tier numbers, we'll post the new math here first. If a vendor cost moves (B2 raises storage pricing, Anthropic changes a tier), you'll see it reflected in this table before it's reflected in the cap.

100 MB is real household scope. Most people inventory specific zones — the garage, the basement storage, the workshop, the moving-day stack, the kids' bins, the holiday decorations — not every drawer in every room. At typical phone-photo sizes that's a few dozen items per zone; drop the resolution and it's hundreds. The real bottleneck isn't item count, it's photo bytes: a high-res phone shot runs 3-6 MB. AI surfaces (item detection, tag suggestions) prefer higher-res inputs but still work on lower-res — accuracy drops gracefully, not catastrophically. Trade-off is yours.

Existing free accounts stay grandfathered — as long as they stay active. If the operator has to shrink the per-tenant cap for NEW signups (because vendor costs moved, or the paying base shrank below what current cap supports), existing free accounts keep their original cap. The pool the operator squeezes is the new-signup pool, not yours.

The one inactivity trade. Free accounts inactive for 180 days get archived: the tenant's data is zipped + uploaded to B2 cold storage (on a separate budget from the active-tier EBS line above) and the local slot freed for new signups. Archived accounts are recoverable — sign back in, the operator triggers restore, your data comes back exactly as you left it. Pro accounts are never archived for inactivity. This is how the free pool stays a real resource rather than silently calcifying into accounts no one's used in years.

If a free user wants more storage, the right answer is Pro. If they can't or don't want to pay, the answer is to delete older photos or re-upload at a lower resolution to make room — same model as a phone's photo library. Either way: your data, your choice, no hostage situation.

What changes as we grow

The ledger above describes today's small-scale deployment. Each step in the user-count arc forces another piece of infrastructure to scale out, and the cost line item for that piece moves up to a new floor. We post the math here so you can see WHY the free tier shifts (or has to shift) as that happens, not just THAT it shifts.

Stage What we add Cost impact
Today (small) Single t3.small + t3.micro staging; SQLite on EBS; B2 for off-site DR; SES for notifications. $30–$50 fixed / mo + per-user B2 + storage marginal cost. Free tier funded out of the Pro remainder once fixed overhead is cleared.
Mid (~100s of users) Bigger prod instance (t3.medium / t3.large); larger EBS volumes; ALB if we go multi-instance; CloudFront for static assets to keep egress flat. $80–$200 fixed / mo. Free tier stays the same per-user (100 MB × $0.008) but cumulative free-tier storage starts pressing on the EBS line. May need to re-balance.
Scale (~1000s of users) Dedicated DB (RDS Postgres, single-AZ → Multi-AZ → with read replicas as load demands). Multiple backend instances behind ALB. Larger storage footprint, possibly tiered (hot EBS + cold S3 for old photos). Caching layer (ElastiCache) if quota-check pressure shows up. $300–$800+ fixed / mo. Per-Pro fixed-cost slice drops as the paying base grows (good); free-tier capacity grows with it because more Pro subs are funding it. We never silently absorb scaling costs — the ledger gets updated and posted here first.

The numbers above are intentionally generous on the upper bound. We'd rather quote a ceiling that turns out to be too high than promise a floor that turns out to be too low. Scale-out tends to happen in steps, not smoothly — each step lands on a new floor and we update the ledger when it does.

Who runs {{ product_name }}

Today the staff is small. We're transparent about who's behind the rails so when something breaks you know which kind of human to expect on the other end.

Role Scope What they do
Maintainer One tenant Customer-side role. Full edit access to one household's inventory: create boxes, accept items, invite read-only members, audit, print labels. That's you (probably).
Read-only member One tenant Customer-side role. Browse access, no edits. Useful for a partner who needs to find things but shouldn't be moving them.
Operator Platform Day-to-day platform staff: handles support tickets, triages feedback, watches usage meters + cost panels, handles deploys, audits the system, helps customers with billing questions or data exports. Never reads tenant content unless explicitly invited by a maintainer.
Admin Platform Business operations: billing setup, vendor contracts, legal, hiring, policy decisions, public communications. Same access constraints as an operator — admin authority is organizational, not data-read.

Operators + admins are platform-level roles backed by an explicit allow-list ({{ "STASH_OPERATOR_EMAILS" }} env var in deploy). Today these collapse to a single human; we're documenting the structure now so when we hire the seam is already there. Spec § "Roles · Operations matrix" describes the access boundaries in code.

Why be this transparent?

Three reasons.

  1. It's how we'd want a service we paid for to behave. The SaaS-pricing default is to obscure the cost stack ("starts at $9!" until you blink twice). We'd rather show our work.
  2. It keeps us honest about scaling. When the vendor bills are visible on a public page, raising prices requires a public reason. When labor allocation is visible, hiring decisions get scrutinized. Both are accountability mechanisms we want.
  3. It's a moat that doesn't run out. A competitor can copy our features, our pricing, our copy. It's harder to copy operating in public. We think users will choose the company that shows them the cost ledger over the one that hides it.

What this page doesn't promise

These numbers are illustrative. We're not committing to a specific cost split per dollar of revenue — actual costs vary by individual usage, vendor pricing changes, and growth stage. What we ARE committing to:

  • We won't quietly raise prices on existing subscribers. Any price change gets 30 days' notice (per the Terms of Service).
  • We won't add an undisclosed sub-processor. Vendor list at /about/sub-processors is the canonical source.
  • We won't ship a "premium" tier above Pro that holds essential features hostage. Pro is the top tier; future higher tiers (if any) add capacity, not access to features Pro users already have.
